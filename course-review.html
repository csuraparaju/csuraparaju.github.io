21-127: Concepts of Mathematics ‚ù§Ô∏è

Professor(s): Clive Newstead

CMU's 21-127 was my first introduction to formal mathematics and proof writing. The course provided me with new perspectives on discrete math and reshaped my view of mathematics as a formal logical system. I found the transition to this formal style challenging but rewarding as it deepened my mathematical reasoning skills. Clive Newstead was an amazing lecturer. During his office hours, he would often go into the history of theorems and proofs, which I found fascinating. Though this course is required for most STEM majors at CMU, I would highly recommend non-STEM majors to take it with Clive since it provides a new perspective on mathematics.

15-122: Principles of Imperative Computation

Professor(s): Iliano Cervesato, Dilsun Kaynar

15-122 was my first computer science class at CMU. It teaches you how to write provably safe and correct programs in a subset of C called C0. The course is very difficult to get an A in as a freshman since a lot of the material is new and it is designed to be challenging. Regardless, I found the course to be rewarding as it taught me how to implement the data structures and algorithms used in the industry in a bare-bones language. The programming assignments are actually interesting, and the final project for the course even requires you to implement a virtual machine in C. Overall, I think 15-122 is a great introduction to computer science, and has the added benefit of making you a well-versed in C right off the bat. Functions are pointers!

15-150: Principles of Functional Programming üåü

Professor(s): Michael Erdmann, Karl Crary

15-150 is CMU's introduction to functional programming course. It is taught in Standard ML, and teaches you how to write provably correct programs using the functional programming paradigm. Unlike 15-122, 15-150 is quite easy to get an A if you take it with Erdmann and Crary. The programming assignments are challenging, but the exams are very reasonable. The course gave me a new perspective on programming, one that I think is very useful for writing correct code. For example, it's interesting to see that since every SML function is recursive, we can prove correctness of our programs using induction. Some of the proofs are very elegant, especially ones relating to functors and modules. I attended Crary's lectures, and he was amazing. Some people thought he went too fast, but that's why I liked him. He was very clear and concise, and his explanations were better than Erdmann's. 15-150 is overall an excellent course, and I would highly recommend it to anyone interested in computer science. Functions are values!

02-251: Great Ideas in Computational Biology ‚ù§Ô∏è

Professor(s): Phillip Compeau

02-251 made me fall in love with Computational Biology, and is one of the main reasons I am working at Mohimani Lab right now. Essentially, the course is a survey of all the pivotal algorithms that gave birth to the field of Computational Biology. From genome sequencing, to systems biology, to even neural networks, 02-251 covers it all. It is a theory heavy class as a lot of the algorithms discussed make use of advanced techniques such as dynamic programming and graph theory. Be prepared to do a lot of proofs about graphs and trees, but also get ready to implement a lot of algorithms from scratch. From the Needleman-Wunsch alignment algorithm to creating multi layer perceptrons, this course will teach you how to implement the most infuential algorithms in Computational Biology. Phillip is an excellent lecturer, and his passion for the subject is evident in his lectures. He is also very approachable and is always willing to help. Although the official pre-reqs for this class are only 15-112 and 21-127/21-128/15-151, I would recommend taking 15-122 either before or concurrently since it will help you with the programming assignments. Whether you are a computer scientist, biologist, chemist, or mathematician, 02-251 is a must take course.

15-251: Great Ideas in Theoretical Computer Science

Professor(s): Anil Ada, Pravesh Kothari

CMU's 15-251 is a must take course for any computer scientist. It is the first rigorous study of computation, and will teach you the importance of mathematics in computer science. The course covers a wide range of topics, from the study of various models of computation such as discrete finite automaton, turing machines and random-access machines, to complexity theory, graph theory and cryptography. It is a very challenging course, but it is also very rewarding. The problem sets are difficult and often requires you to apply topics learned from lecture in a new way. Homeworks are done in the form of weekly writing sessions, where you have to reproduce proofs to selected homework problems independently in an exam-like setting. The exams are also challenging, but they are fair. Both Anil Ada and Pravesh Kothari are engaging lecturers, and they are both very approachable. The TAs are also incredibly helpful.

11-485: Introduction to Deep Learning üåü

Professor(s): Bhiksha Raj

11-481 covers the basics of neural networks, from multi layer perceptrons to convolutional and recurrent neural networks. The course is very hands-on, and you will get to implement all the essential algorithms in deep learning such as gradient descent and backpropagation from scracth. Each homework is split into two parts, part one and part two. Part one of an assignment asks you to implement a deep learning library akin to PyTorch from scratch using python and numpy. The cumulation of all the part ones is a fully functional deep learning library that you can use to train your own neural networks. Part two of an assignment is more hands on and asks you to use existing frameworks like PyTorch to implement a neural network to solve a real world problem. For example, Homework 2 part 2 was to implement a Convolutional Neural Netowrk for recognizing previously seen faces. Bhiksha is an engaging professor, although he can be a bit much sometimes. He often has class on holidays, and even assigns homework due during university wide breaks.

21-325: Probability Theory

Professor(s): Agoston Pisztora

Pisztora's 21-325 is an introductory to probability theory course. It covers the basics of probability theory, from random variables to the central limit theorem. The course is proof heavy, and requires you to be comfortable with infinite series and sequences. The homeworks are often easy and sometimes require you to perform even mindless computation. The exams, are not easy and there's always one question that is challenging. Pisztora puts a heavy emphasis on discrete random variables in the first half of the course, causing it to be a bit boring. It is clear that Pisztora does not prepare for his lectures, and often makes mistakes. Overall, I would not recommend this course unless you are a math major.

03-151: Honors Modern Biology üåü

Professor(s): Jonathan Minden

Taking Honors Modern Biology as a freshman was one of the highlights of my first year in college. I found the subject material fascinating, especially learning about the intricate molecular processes that enable biological systems to function. The accelerated pacing gave Minden to dive deeper into cell biology and molecular concepts, which I loved. Getting to spend more class periods exploring the molecular side of biology was engaging and helped cement foundational knowledge. Professor Miden's infectious passion for biology was obvious during his lively lectures. His enthusiasm brought the material to life and inspired me to pursue biology coursework. Though challenging, Honors Modern Biology provided a great introductory experience that stimulated my interest in the subject. The course offered a rigorous yet captivating overview of key biological concepts, setting me up well for more advanced study, and giving me an excellent background in biology when working on computational biology projects.

09-107: Honors Modern Chemistry

Professor(s): Gizelle Sherwood

The Chemistry department's Honors introduction to Chemistry course is not as enjoyable as Honor's Biology. The topics covered during lectures are mostly AP Chemistry review, but the exams are much more difficult. Be ready to learn a lot of organic chemistry and physical chemistry! The course is challenging, but it is not impossible to get an A. The exams are very hard, but there is plenty of extra credit opportunity. However, what made the course not as enjoyable, thought, was Dr. Sherwood's classes. Her lectures were often confusing, and worst of all, she was very dismissive of your questions. Often, she would move past a topic without explaining it well, and if you asked her to clarify, she would say something along the lines of "you should know this already". The TAs were very helpful, though, and I would recommend going to their office hours. Overall, I would not recommend this course unless you are a chemistry major.

21-241: Matricies and Linear Transformations

Professor(s): Elisa Bellah

This is a standard course in linear algebra, especially when taken with Dr. Bellah. It covers the basics, from vector spaces to linear transformations and eigen-things. It's proof heavy with Dr. Bellah, with little to no computation. Believe it or not, she allowed us to use online calculators for row reducing as she wanted us to focus on the theory. Overall, it is pretty easy to get an A in this class because of how generous the grading is. Although the exams are all proofs, they are very straightforward and are not designed to trick you. This course with Dr. Bellah is great if you want to get any pre-requisites out of the way, but I would not recommend it if you want to gain a deeper understanding of linear algebra. Instead, I suggest you take 21-241 with Dr. Jason Howell, or even 21-242.

21-259: Calculus in 3D

Professor(s): Evan O'dorney

This is a standard, run of the mill, multivariable calculus course. It covers the basics, from 3D vectors to partial derivatives and multiple integrals. The course is very computation heavy, and requires you to be comfortable with calculus 1. Professor O'dorney is a new hire for CMU and it is clear that he is still getting used to teaching. He does not have practice material for his exams, and his homeworks are sometimes tricky. However, he is very approachable and is always willing to help. Overall, I would recommend this course if you want to get any pre-requisites out of the way, but I would not recommend it if you want to gain a deeper understanding of multivariable calculus. Instead, I suggest you take 21-268 or 21-269.

21-259: Principles of Microeconomics

Professor(s): James Best

Great course to fill a general education requirement. It covers the basics of microeconomics, and is quite easy.
