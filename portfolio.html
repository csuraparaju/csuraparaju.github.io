UChicago Trading Competition



Going into the UChicago Trading Competition, I had zero experience with algorithmic trading. It was completely new territory for me, but being selected as one of around 150 students to participate was an awesome opportunity to learn.

I worked with my team for about 3 weeks and came up with a pairs trading algorithm for trading seven correlated assets. This was a form of statistical arbitrage that computed fair values for a set of assets based on a rolling window of historical price data. The algorithm would identify potential trading opportunities when the prices of these assets deviated from their expected "mean", and execute trades to capitalize on the eventual convergence back to equilibrium.

Algorithm for RNA Structure Prediction



I worked with in a team of four to produce a novel algorithm for predicting the secondary structure of RNA, a famous open problem in computer science. In RNA secondary structure prediction, base-pair probabilities refer to the likelihood of two nucleotides (bases) in an RNA sequence forming a bond. These probabilities provide valuable about the potential secondary structure of the RNA molecule.

My teammates worked on developing a convolutional neural network that can take in a sequence of RNA string, and output the base pair probabilities. However, the probabilities alone do not directly translate into the full secondary structure sequence. So, I worked on a dynamic programming algorithm could decode and construct the most likely secondary structure. It did this by identifying the optimal combination of base pairs that maximized the overall probability of the sequence. This approach allowed us to leverage the predictive power of the neural network while incorporating algorithmic techniques to derive the final RNA secondary structure predictions accurately.

For the past couple of months, I've been working on a high performance deep learning library written in C++, similar to PyTorch.
